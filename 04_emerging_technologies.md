The Purpose of Internet of Things 

The Internet of Things (IoT) is a network of physical devices such as appliances, vehicles, wearables, and industrial machines that have sensors, software, and network connections so they can collect, share, and act on data. These “smart objects” can be as simple as a thermostat in your house or as complex as smart cars and factory equipment, and they are able to talk to each other and other internet-connected devices. IoT is used in many ways, from monitoring conditions on farms and managing traffic in cities to controlling machines in factories and keeping track of inventory in warehouses. Some even see it as the foundation for entire smart cities in the future.



The purpose of IoT is pretty simple. It is meant to make life easier, work more efficient, and systems smarter. By connecting everyday things to the internet, we can automate tasks, make decisions based on real-time data, and even fix problems before they happen. Whether it is adjusting your home temperature while you are out, predicting when a machine needs a repair, or helping traffic flow better, IoT takes regular “things” and turns them into quiet, behind-the-scenes helpers. At the end of the day, the main goal is to use that connectivity and automation to make everything run smoother and smarter, both at home and on a much bigger scale.



The Usage of Internet of Things – Why IoT Matters in Education

The Internet of Things plays an important role in making education more accessible, interactive, and collaborative. It allows students and teachers to connect in real time, extending the classroom beyond physical walls and into cyberspace. Even with the return of face-to-face classes after the pandemic, IoT continues to enhance lessons by making them more engaging and by simplifying group projects from planning to prototyping.



More importantly, IoT in education equips students with the skills, knowledge, and tools they will need in the future. As IoT becomes an increasingly integrated part of everyday life, introducing it early ensures students are prepared to thrive in a connected world. 



Example: RFID-Based Smart Student Arrival System

A real-world example of IoT application in education can be seen in Shenzhen Fuqiao Senior High School, which has implemented an RFID-based student arrival system. This system uses NFC-enabled ID cards to automatically record students’ arrival times as they enter the campus. The data is instantly processed and displayed in a campus management interface accessible to administrators, teachers, parents, and students.



With the rapid development of IoT technology and the integration of big data, such systems have become an important direction in building smart campuses. In this case, the RFID solution does more than just track attendance—it processes large volumes of data to identify patterns, generate useful insights, and provide a user-friendly interface tailored to the needs of different stakeholders. This allows for real-time monitoring, accurate record-keeping, and enhanced campus operations, improving communication between schools and parents while also supporting more efficient administrative processes.
